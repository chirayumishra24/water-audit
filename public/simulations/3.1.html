From Raw Numbers to Clear Insights

You’ve measured flow rates, counted containers, interviewed officials — now it’s time to organize everything. Raw numbers are just noise until they’re in a table. Use a notebook, Google Sheets, or Excel to spot patterns, calculate totals, and see what matters most.

Goal: Turn “I think I use a lot of water” into “I use 72 liters per day — and showers are 62% of it!”

The Master Data Table

Build a simple table like this to combine all your measurements. It shows every activity, how much water it uses per time, how often you do it, and the daily total.

Date Activity Tool Used Volume per Use (L) Frequency (Times/Day) Total Daily Volume (L)
02/03 Drinking 750 mL Bottle 0.75 L 4 3.0 L
02/03 Shower Flow Rate (9 L/min) 45.0 L (5 min) 1 45.0 L
02/03 Dishwashing Kitchen Sink 12.0 L 2 24.0 L
Total         72.0 L

Tip: Add more rows for brushing teeth, toilet flushes, laundry, gardening, etc. The more activities you track, the clearer the picture!

Setting Up Your Spreadsheet

If you use Google Sheets or Excel, let the computer do the math! Create these columns in your “Flow Rate” sheet:

  1. Column A (Volume): Enter the bucket size in liters (e.g., 5)
  2. Column B (Time): Enter seconds to fill (e.g., 20)
  3. Column C (Flow Rate L/min): Use this formula:
    =(A2/B2)*60
    Drag the formula down for all rows — instant results!

Pro Tip: In another sheet, use =SUM() to add up daily totals automatically. Create a pie chart to see which activity uses the most water!

Data Visualization: From Liters to Graphs

A picture is worth a thousand numbers! After collecting a week of data:

  • Create a Pie Chart — shows which activity uses the biggest “slice” of your water (usually shower, toilet, laundry)
  • Look for spikes — did usage jump on Saturday? Maybe car washing or guests
  • Compare days — spot patterns (more on weekends? less in rainy season?)

The “Big Three” usually eat most water: showers, toilets, washing clothes. Fixing these gives the biggest savings!
