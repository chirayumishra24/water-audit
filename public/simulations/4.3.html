💧 Save Water at the Tap
  • 🪠 Aerators = less water, same feel

  • 🌧️ Recharge groundwater = more water for all

Use Less at the Tap — Save More Overall

Fixing leaks stops waste — but reducing flow at every tap is another powerful way to save water. Most people open taps fully (“full throttle”), using far more than needed. Simple devices and habits can cut usage dramatically without losing convenience.

Water-Saving Devices: Aerators & Flow Caps

These cheap, easy-to-install attachments reduce flow while keeping the stream strong and comfortable.

Result: Cut tap flow from 15 L/min to 6 L/min → save up to 60% on hand-washing, dishwashing, and showers!

Practical Implementation & Mindful Habits

Devices help, but behavior matters too — you can save water today without buying anything.

  • Manual Control: Open taps only partially for hand-washing, brushing, or utensils — often enough for the job.
  • Before & After Measurement: Use bucket + stopwatch to measure flow rate (L/min) before and after changes — see the real difference!

30-second hand wash at full flow = ~7.5 L
At half flow or with aerator = ~3 L
→ Saves 4.5 L every time!

Increasing Water Availability: Groundwater Recharge

Groundwater (wells & borewells) is vital — but over-extraction is depleting it fast. Simple community actions can help rainwater soak back into the ground instead of running off.

Check Dam – Slows water flow to allow recharge

Percolation Tank / Borewell Recharge

  1. Contour Bunds — Earth barriers along contour lines on slopes hold water, reduce runoff, allow percolation, and prevent soil erosion.
  2. Check Dams — Small dams across streams slow water speed, giving it more time to soak into the ground and recharge aquifers.
  3. Percolation Tanks & Borewell Recharge — Man-made basins or direct recharge into borewells capture runoff and send it underground.

Other ways to increase water: plant trees (more rain), reduce pollution, stop wastage, recycle water, and harvest rainwater.
