<Conversation>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">

We’ve discussed the water crisis and the power of data. Today, we put that into action with a Water Audit.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Aisha">
Like a financial audit, but for water?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Exactly! It’s a systematic assessment that tracks water from inflow to discharge. The goal is to quantify usage, find leaks, and recommend ways to conserve water and save money.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Kavya">
How do we actually conduct one?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
We follow a structured, four-step approach:
1. **Collect Data**: Gather facts on current water use.
2. **Identify Wastage**: Inspect taps and pipes to find leaks or careless use.
3. **Analyze & Interpret**: Organize the data to understand consumption patterns.
4. **Apply Strategies**: Develop real-world solutions to stop the waste.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Rohan">
So, once we fix the leaks, the audit is over?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Not quite. An audit has **Predictive Power**. Just like meteorologists use past data to predict the monsoon, we use current consumption data to estimate a community's future water needs.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Aisha">
But that prediction is only useful if our numbers are accurate, right?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Spot on. That requires **Data Integrity**. Our data must be correct, authentic, and reliable—meaning if anyone else repeats the audit, they should arrive at the exact same results.
</Message>

</Conversation>
