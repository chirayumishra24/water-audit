### Why boundaries matter

An audit boundary decides what counts. If that boundary is vague, the final findings will also be vague.

You need to know whether the audit covers:

- the full facility
- one building
- one production line
- one support system such as cooling, cleaning, or utilities

Without this decision, inputs and outputs are hard to reconcile.

### Two kinds of scope

### Physical scope

This is the real-world area or process under review. It might be a campus, factory, treatment unit, or a single high-consumption department.

### Analytical scope

This is the level of detail you plan to reach. For example, you might include the whole site physically but only analyze the largest water-using systems in detail.

Both scopes need to be stated explicitly.

### What to include in a boundary statement

A good boundary statement names:

- the entry points for water
- the main storage or distribution elements
- the major use points
- the discharge or reuse destinations
- any known exclusions

If a leased area, contractor process, or neighboring utility feed is excluded, say so early.

### Breaking the site into subsystems

A large site is easier to audit when divided into smaller water pathways. Typical subsystems include:

- process water
- domestic water
- cooling systems
- boiler feed
- washdown or cleaning
- landscaping or outdoor use
- wastewater and reuse loops

This decomposition creates the structure used later in [the site walkthrough chapter](/modules/measurement/site-walkthrough-and-loss-points/).

### Common beginner mistakes

### Boundary too broad

When everything is included but nothing is subdivided, the audit becomes descriptive rather than diagnostic.

### Boundary too narrow

When the team audits only one visible process, it may miss the supporting systems where losses actually occur.

### Boundary mismatch

This happens when the billing meter, operating logs, and field observations all refer to different system extents.

### A practical rule

Start with the smallest boundary that still contains the decision you need to make.

If the goal is to reduce washdown demand, the scope does not need to begin with every water use on site. If the goal is site-wide loss reduction, the boundary must be broader.

### Deliverable from this chapter

Before you collect measurements, prepare a simple boundary note with:

1. audit purpose
2. included areas or systems
3. excluded areas or systems
4. subsystem list
5. main source and discharge points

That note becomes the reference point for every later chapter.
