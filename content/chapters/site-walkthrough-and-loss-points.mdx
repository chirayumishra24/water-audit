## Why the walkthrough still matters

Even a strong water balance has blind spots. A field walkthrough reveals the physical and behavioral details that numbers can hide.

This is where the auditor checks whether the system behaves the way the documents suggest.

## What to observe

During the walkthrough, focus on places where water can be:

- introduced
- stored
- transferred
- intentionally discharged
- unintentionally lost

Typical attention points include tanks, cooling loops, washdown zones, hose stations, drain channels, and control valves.

## Observable signs of loss

Look for evidence such as:

- persistent wet areas
- overflow marks or splash staining
- frequently running make-up water
- hoses left open or poorly controlled
- manual bypasses that have become routine practice

These are small observations, but they often explain large cumulative volumes over time.

## Process behavior matters

A system may be technically sound yet still waste water because of how people use it. That is why operator interviews are part of the walkthrough.

Useful prompts include:

- What happens during startup?
- What happens during product changeover?
- When does cleaning water use peak?
- Which valves are adjusted manually?
- Which problems do operators already know about?

The answers often provide context that no meter trend can reveal alone.

## Record the site logically

Do not collect observations as isolated notes. Tie each one back to:

- subsystem
- location
- suspected mechanism
- likely impact
- evidence type

That structure makes later prioritization much easier.

## Output from the walkthrough

At the end of this chapter, you should have a short list of candidate loss points. Some will be confirmed issues. Others will remain hypotheses until quantified.

That is acceptable. The goal is not to prove everything immediately. The goal is to narrow the search and prepare for action ranking.
