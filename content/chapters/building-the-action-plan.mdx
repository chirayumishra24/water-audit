## The plan is the real handoff

The audit report may be informative, but the action plan is what the site team actually uses. It should be clear enough that the next responsible person knows what to do without re-reading the entire audit.

## What every action needs

Each recommended action should include:

- the issue being addressed
- the intended result
- the accountable owner
- the expected timeline
- the success metric
- any dependency or prerequisite

This turns audit findings into managed work.

## Balance the portfolio of actions

A credible plan usually includes a mix of:

- immediate operational corrections
- maintenance tasks
- meter or monitoring improvements
- medium-term process changes
- capital items requiring separate approval

That balance is important because it shows the site where progress can start now while larger decisions are still being prepared.

## Define success clearly

Examples of useful success measures include:

- reduced baseline night flow
- lower make-up water to a defined subsystem
- reduced overflow incidents
- improved alignment between sub-meter totals and main-meter totals

Success should be observable in data, field condition, or both.

## Set the review loop

An action plan should not end at issue assignment. It should also define when the team will review:

- implementation status
- measured result
- remaining uncertainty
- need for follow-up audit work

That closes the loop between discovery and continuous improvement.

## Final note

At this point, the learner has moved from purpose to boundary, from balance to field verification, and from findings to execution. That is the intended shape of the course and the intended shape of a practical water audit.
