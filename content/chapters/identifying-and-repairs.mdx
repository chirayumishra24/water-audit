### Part 1: Identifying and Measuring Wastage

<Conversation>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
We know how to plan an audit, but where do we actually start looking for water loss in our homes or school? The first step is identifying wastage.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Kavya">
I usually just look for dripping taps. Is there more to it?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Yes! Besides dripping taps, you should look for wet patches on walls or the ground, which indicate hidden pipe leaks. You should also check overhead tanks—faulty ball valves often cause them to overflow, wasting thousands of liters.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Rohan">
Once we find a leak, how do we measure the exact loss?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
You can quantify it using just a bucket and a stopwatch. Place the bucket under the leak, start the timer for exactly one minute, and measure the collected water in a 1-liter bottle. Multiply that volume to cover 24 hours, and you'll see the daily loss. Remember, just a running tap for five minutes wastes 45 liters!
</Message>

</Conversation>

  <ol className="space-y-4 text-slate-700">
    <li><strong>1. Preparation:</strong> Always turn off the water mains first.</li>
    <li><strong>2. Disassembly:</strong> Use a spanner to remove the tap's cover and handle. Unscrew the bonnet and remove the headgear.</li>
    <li><strong>3. Replacing Parts:</strong> The jumper valve will fall out. You simply replace the old body washer, O-ring, and jumper valve with new ones.</li>
    <li><strong>4. Lubrication & Reassembly:</strong> Apply tap lubricant to the spindle, refit the bonnet (don't over-tighten!), and turn the mains back on to check your work.</li>
  </ol>
</div>

### Part 3: Flow Restriction & Increasing Availability

<Conversation>

<Message speaker="Rohan">
Even if taps aren't leaking, I notice people have a habit of opening them at "full throttle" to wash their hands. Isn't that a huge waste?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
It is. That's why, alongside fixing leaks, we use Flow Restriction. We can install small devices to manage the volume.
</Message>

</Conversation>

<div className="bg-blue-50 p-8 rounded-3xl border-2 border-blue-100 my-12">
  <h3 className="text-2xl font-bold text-blue-900 mb-6">Flow Restriction Devices</h3>
  <img src="https://login.skillizee.io/s/articles/69ef3fcfcf1df8147748719e/images/image-20260427170351-31.png" width="627" height="205" alt="Aerators" className="rounded-xl mb-6 mx-auto" />
  
  <ul className="space-y-4">
    <li><strong>Aerators:</strong> Small attachments screwed onto the faucet tip. They mix air with the water, giving you high pressure while actually using much less water.</li>
    <li><strong>Flow Caps:</strong> Physical restrictors inside the tap or showerhead that limit the maximum liters per minute.</li>
  </ul>
  
  <div className="mt-6">
    <YoutubeEmbed url="https://www.youtube.com/shorts/cpdgNaYevK0" title="How Aerators Work" />
  </div>
</div>

We don't even need devices for that, right? We can just build a habit of opening the tap halfway.

Exactly, Kavya. Behavioral changes are just as powerful. You can measure the "Water Flow Rate" before and after changing your habits to see the difference.

<Message speaker="Aisha">
So if we stop the wastage, fix the leaks, and use aerators, are we solving the crisis?
</Message>

We are doing our part to conserve! But securing our water future requires a combined effort. Alongside reducing waste and recycling water, we must actively work to increase availability through methods like rainwater harvesting, reducing pollution, and planting trees.
