### The role of the balance

Once the scope is defined, the next job is to build a numerical picture of the system. This is the water balance.

In its simplest form:

`Input = Useful use + losses + discharge + change in storage`

The exact categories may vary by site, but the principle stays the same: every meaningful flow must have a place in the model.

### Start with a time period

Choose a period that fits the operation:

- daily for unstable or batch processes
- weekly for operational pattern review
- monthly for utility alignment and broad facility analysis

The period must be consistent across all data sources. If one source is monthly and another is daily, normalize them before comparing.

### Typical balance buckets

Most beginner audits begin with these categories:

| Category | Typical examples |
| --- | --- |
| Inputs | municipal supply, borewell extraction, tanker supply, recycled inflow |
| Productive use | process water, cleaning, cooling, boiler feed |
| Intended output | product moisture, discharge, reuse transfer |
| Losses | leaks, overflow, excessive purge, untracked drain loss |
| Storage change | tank level shifts across the chosen period |

This table is only a starting structure. The actual site may require finer detail.

### What a gap means

If the left and right side of the balance do not align, the missing part is the gap. That gap usually points to one of three things:

1. missing measurements
2. wrong assumptions
3. real loss

The audit should resist jumping to the third explanation too quickly.

### From total balance to subsystem balance

A site-wide balance is useful, but it is rarely enough to guide action. Once the total picture exists, repeat the same logic for high-use subsystems.

For example:

- cooling tower loop
- process wash line
- domestic and sanitation network
- irrigation or landscape consumption

This layered approach reduces the chance that one large category hides multiple different problems.

### Practical interpretation

Suppose total monthly input is stable, but production output falls. Water intensity rises even before any leak is confirmed. That may still trigger action because the audit is interested in performance, not only failure.

Likewise, if discharge remains unusually high compared with production load, the balance can direct the audit toward purge settings, blowdown practices, or cleaning routines.
