Understanding where your water comes from is the final piece of the puzzle. Moving from "how much I use" to "where it starts" connects your household habits to the larger ecosystem. Engaging with local water supply officials or visiting a treatment plant can turn a utility bill into a story of engineering and environment.

Before conducting an interview, it helps to understand the typical infrastructure. Most communities rely on a mix of surface water and groundwater.

- **Primary Sources:** Reservoirs (dams), rivers, or underground aquifers.
- **Storage:** Water towers or high-level tanks that use gravity to maintain pressure in your pipes.
- Water received from rains comes to us through natural as well as manmade water sources—rivers, wells, borewells, tanks, etc. It gets stored in big and small dams. Water from dams or storage tanks comes to us through canals, pipelines, etc. Knowing where your water comes to you and how much population is dependent on this water is necessary for your audit.

### Conducting the Interview: Key Questions

When speaking with a water official or civil engineer, use these targeted questions to get the most insightful data. The water supply to our homes is managed through the Gram Panchayat office, Municipal Corporations, water supply department, ward office, and similar bodies. You can visit these offices to understand the water supply system of your locality.

### Activity: Comparison and Analysis

In the water audit project, you will compare the average consumption data you collected first-hand (primary data) against prescribed consumption standards provided by government initiatives like the Jal Jeevan Mission.

### Comparison Standards for Water Usage

To analyze your findings, use the following prescribed standards as a benchmark for daily water use per person:

- **Rural Areas:** 55 Liters (L) per day.
- **Urban Areas:** 135 Liters (L) per day.

You can use a table to organize this comparison and identify if the consumption in your study area aligns with national goals.

### Record of Average Consumption versus Prescribed Consumption

| Category | Data/Standard | Water available per person per day as per secondary data |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Primary Data** | Average consumption per person per day (calculated from your family surveys) | |
| **Secondary Data** | Water available per person per day (based on your geographical and rainfall calculations) | |
| **Prescribed Standard** | 55 L for rural areas / 135 L for urban areas (Jal Jeevan Mission) | |

### Key Considerations for Analysis

- **Reliability:** When using secondary data (like standards from the Press Information Bureau or Ministry of Jal Shakti), always mention the source and the year to ensure the information is current and reliable.
- **Identifying High Consumption:** Use this comparison to identify families or areas that consume more water than the average or prescribed standard. Investigate the reasons behind this, such as specific lifestyles or undetected leakages.
- **Water Scarcity:** This analysis helps you understand if the rainwater and supply availability in your district are sufficient to meet these prescribed needs.

Suppose you want to refer to secondary data. The person who is listening to you, or reading a report you have written, must know where the data is from so that they can refer to it. They would also like to be assured that the data is reliable.

Since data keeps on changing (e.g., population numbers change, climatic parameters change), you must mention the year the data was reported. In which case, for example, you will say/write as follows: The Government of India, through the Jal Jeevan Mission, is trying to provide 55 L of tap water per day for rural populations.

This secondary data has been obtained from a release of the Press Information Bureau of India dated 14 Aug 2024, which is available on the website: https://pib.gov.in/PressNoteDetails.aspx?NoteId=152025&ModuleId=3

If you are giving information from a book or report, then you must mention the name of the book or title of the report, and who has published it and in which year.

Now, try to obtain data from similar reliable sources. You can search the library, newspaper reports, magazines and publications to get the data. Alternatively, use online search with following key words:

- Rainfall + Year + name of district
- Population + name of the district + census
- Water availability per person + name of the district
- Ministry of Statistics + Govt of India + Water
- NITI Aayog + water report

### Activity: Rainwater Availability in Your District

**Secondary Data Collection**

Recording data to estimate rainwater availability:

| Examples | About your district/town/village/ward | Reference/source of information | What does the data tell us? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Population:** District/Town/Village/Ward (e.g., Pune District: 9,429,408) | | | |
| **Area of District/Town:** (e.g., Pune District: 15,643 square km) | | | |
| **Rainfall of the district in the year** (e.g., Pune District rainfall in 2023: 446 mm ≈ 0.446m) | | | |
| **Total water availability:** Area in m² × Rainfall in m (1 m³ = 1000 L; 1 km² = 1,000,000 m²) Total water availability in Pune District: 15643 × 10⁶ × 0.446 = 6977 × 10⁶ m³ | calculation | | |
| **Domestic rainwater availability:** Generally, 70% for agriculture, 20% for industry, 10% for domestic consumption. Pune example: 697.7 × 10⁶ × 1000 L → Per capita: 73,991 L → **Per day: 202 L/day** | Calculation | | Rainwater available for different purposes |

The above information will help you understand rainwater availability, usage and scarcity in your geography. Calculating water availability based on a district's geography and rainfall may not give you an accurate picture. All rainwater flows into rivers. The entire population, from the origin of the river up to the point where the river meets the sea, depends on river water. Some of these areas have high rainfall, and some have less rainfall. However, the water in the river basin belongs to the entire population.
