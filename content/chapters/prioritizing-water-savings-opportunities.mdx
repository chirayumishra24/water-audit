## Findings are not the final output

After a walkthrough and data review, the audit usually produces more issues than a team can address at once. Prioritization is the step that turns observation into management focus.

Without it, even a good audit can stall.

## Four useful ranking lenses

### Impact

How much water, cost, or compliance risk could this action address?

### Effort

How difficult is the action to implement operationally?

### Confidence

How certain is the evidence behind the finding?

### Urgency

Does the issue create immediate operational, environmental, or permit-related concern?

## Typical opportunity groups

Most findings fall into one of these groups:

- operational quick wins
- maintenance corrections
- measurement improvements
- process optimization
- capital projects

Grouping this way helps teams match opportunities to the right owners.

## Quick win does not mean low value

A small operational change, such as tightening washdown duration or correcting overflow control, can outperform a larger capital idea in the short term.

Likewise, a metering improvement may not save water directly, but it can unlock better control across several systems.

## Example ranking logic

If two findings have similar water impact, choose the one with:

- higher confidence
- lower implementation effort
- faster operational benefit

If a high-impact item has low confidence, the next action may be a focused measurement or pilot rather than immediate full implementation.

## What the shortlist should contain

For each shortlisted action, prepare:

- clear problem statement
- expected benefit
- likely owner
- required next step
- confidence note

That set becomes the starting material for the final action plan.
