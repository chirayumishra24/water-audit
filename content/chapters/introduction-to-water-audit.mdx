<Message speaker="Rohan">
And we're pumping out groundwater faster than it can refill, right?

<img src="https://login.skillizee.io/s/articles/69ef3fcfcf1df8147748719e/images/image-20260427162242-3.png" width="600" height="449" alt="Groundwater crisis" className="rounded-lg shadow-md my-4" />
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Exactly. We are the world's largest user of groundwater, yet 70% of it is contaminated. Out of 122 countries, India ranks 120th in water quality.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Kavya">
But aren't droughts just natural disasters?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Weather plays a part, but human actions—like deforestation, over-extraction, and waste—drive the crisis. Remember, leaving a tap running for five minutes wastes 45 litres of water, while others walk miles for just one bucket.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Rohan">
The problem feels too massive to fix. Where do we even start?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
We start with Data. It is our most strategic asset. For example, by tracking weather data, we can warn farmers of a dry summer so they can plant crops that require less water—saving their livelihoods.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Aisha">
Where does this data come from?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
Two main sources:
*   **Primary Data:** First-hand collection, like surveying village wells or running field tests.
*   **Secondary Data:** Existing reliable sources, like government reports.
</Message>

<Message speaker="Kavya">
How do we know the numbers are actually trustworthy?
</Message>

<Message speaker="Mr. Sharma">
That requires Data Integrity. To be useful, data must be correct, authentic, reliable, and consistent. If our data lacks integrity, our solutions to the water crisis will ultimately fail.
</Message>
